Introduction

Over the course of the semester, I’ve gained a much better understanding of what technical communication actually means in practice. Going into this class, my main goal was to get better at explaining complex or technical ideas in a way that still feels clear and relevant to the person reading it. Across my projects, I focused on making my work audience-aware, easy to understand, and practical.

Looking back, I believe I have improved a lot in how I think about communication. Instead of just writing to complete an assignment, I think about who I am writing for, what they care about, and how they’ll actually use what I create. This analysis shows how I worked toward the four learning outcomes: rhetoric, process, modes and media, and design.

Rhetoric

One of the main things I improved on was being more intentional about how I write for different audiences. Each project had a completely different audience, so I had to adjust how I communicated for each one.

In my job materials, I focused on presenting myself in a way that would help me stand out to employers. Instead of just listing what I did, I framed my accomplishments around impact. For example, I described my Bank of America internship as supporting “over 100,000 projects across 8 lines of business,” which sounds much more meaningful than just saying I worked on a platform. I also made sure my bullet points quantified results, like reducing manual work by “over 20 hours per week.”

For my Character.AI crisis report, my tone was completely different. Instead of selling myself, I was analyzing a company’s communication and pointing out what they did wrong. For example, I wrote that the company “used conditional language, shifted responsibility onto users, and displayed safety messaging in areas of low visibility.” That required me to be more critical and precise, especially when talking about responsibility and risk.

Then, in my RSO onboarding guide, I had to think about usability more than anything. The audience is student leaders who are busy and probably skimming, so I made the tone direct and practical. I used clear instructions like “Submit requests early” and added TL;DR summaries so people could get what they needed quickly.

Overall, this shows a shift in how I communicated depending on the situation, which is something I did not do as intentionally before this class.

Process

Another big area of growth for me was how I approached revision. Earlier on, I would complete tasks more linearly and move on, but this semester I spent more time going back and making revisions based on feedback and what I realized was not working.

A good example is my Character.AI infographic. At first, the infographic was very text-heavy and felt more like a paper than a visual asset. After revising, I reorganized everything into clearer sections and added more visuals to show how emotional attachment forms between users and AI. That made the ideas easier for the audience to understand and more engaging.

For the RSO onboarding guide, I also made structural changes after realizing how people would actually use it. I added TL;DR summaries and broke the guide into sections and subsections, such as “Submitting a Budget Request” and “How to Reserve a Space.” This made it feel more like a real resource instead of a long document.

Even in my resume, I revised my bullet points to follow a more consistent structure of action plus impact. This consistency made everything clearer and more professional.

Overall, I started thinking more about communication as something you improve and iterate over time, not just something you submit once and are done with.

Modes and Media

This class also helped me get more comfortable using different formats to communicate ideas, not just writing.

My Character.AI project is probably the best example of this. I did not just write a report; I also created an infographic that shows the timeline of events and how users form emotional attachments to AI. For example, I included statistics like “93% of users engage in companion-like behavior” and visualized the cycle of interaction and emotional validation. That made the information easier for the audience to understand and more engaging than text alone.

For the RSO onboarding guide, I focused on making the PDF interactive and easy to navigate. I used headings, links, images, and structured sections so users could jump to what they needed instead of reading everything straight through.

Overall, I’ve gotten much better at thinking about how information is delivered, not just what I’m saying.

Design

Design is something I had not thought much about before this class, but now I see how important it is for making information actually usable.

In my Character.AI infographic, I used layout and structure to guide the reader. The timeline at the top gives context first, then the statistics, then the cycle diagram showing the bigger pattern. This flow helps the reader take in the information without being overwhelmed.

In my RSO onboarding guide, I focused a lot on readability. I used headers, bullet points, spacing, and TL;DR sections to make the guide easier to skim. This makes a huge difference because people are more likely to actually use the guide.

For my resume, I kept the design clean, simple, and easy to scan quickly, which is important for recruiters.

Now I think about design less as “making things look nice” and more as making information easier to use and understand.

Conclusion

Overall, I feel like I’ve grown a lot as a technical communicator this semester. I’m more aware of my audience, more intentional about how I present information, and more willing to revise and improve my work.

The biggest shift for me was thinking about communication from the user’s perspective. Whether it is a recruiter, a student leader, or a general audience reading about AI, I try to think about what they need and how to make that information as clear as possible.

This mindset is something I’ll carry into my career, especially in software engineering, where being able to communicate clearly is just as important as writing code.
